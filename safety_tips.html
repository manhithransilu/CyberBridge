The "Core 4" Protective Habits

In the complex world of cybersecurity, it's easy to feel overwhelmed by the sheer number of threats and protective measures. That's why we've distilled essential security practices into the "Core 4" — four fundamental habits that, when practiced consistently, dramatically reduce your risk of falling victim to cyber attacks.

These habits are designed specifically for university students who may be managing multiple accounts, using various devices, and accessing sensitive academic resources. Master these four practices, and you'll have a solid foundation for your digital security.

Master These Four Habits

Strong Passphrases

Why it matters: Weak passwords are the leading cause of account compromises. Attackers use automated tools that can crack simple passwords in seconds, but strong passphrases can take centuries to break.

What to do: Instead of using complex passwords with random symbols (which are hard to remember), use passphrases — long phrases made of random words. For example, "correct-horse-battery-staple" is both memorable and extremely secure.

Practical Tips:

  • Length over complexity: A 20-character passphrase with common words is stronger than an 8-character password with symbols
  • Use a password manager: Tools like Bitwarden (free), 1Password, or LastPass can generate and store unique passphrases for every account
  • Never reuse passwords: If one account is breached, all accounts with the same password become vulnerable
  • Change passwords if compromised: Use Have I Been Pwned to check if your email has appeared in data breaches
Student Tip: Your university account password should be unique and never used for any other service!

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Why it matters: Even the strongest password can be stolen through phishing or data breaches. MFA adds an additional verification step, so even if an attacker has your password, they still can't access your account without the second factor.

What to do: Enable MFA on every account that offers it, especially email, banking, social media, and your university accounts. Most services now offer this feature in their security settings.

Types of MFA (from most to least secure):

  • Hardware security keys: Physical devices like YubiKey — most secure but requires purchasing a device
  • Authenticator apps: Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, or Authy — generate time-based codes
  • Push notifications: Approve logins from your phone — convenient and reasonably secure
  • SMS codes: Better than nothing, but vulnerable to SIM swapping attacks — use other methods when available

How to Enable MFA:

  1. Go to your account's security or privacy settings
  2. Look for "Two-Factor Authentication" or "Two-Step Verification"
  3. Choose your preferred method and follow the setup instructions
  4. Save your backup codes in a secure location
Important: Always save your MFA backup codes! If you lose access to your phone, these codes are your only way back into your accounts.

Phishing Recognition

Why it matters: Phishing attacks trick you into revealing sensitive information by impersonating trusted entities. They're the most common attack vector against students, often disguised as messages from the university, financial aid, or popular services.

What to do: Always verify before you click. Legitimate organisations rarely ask for sensitive information via email, and urgent or threatening language is a major red flag.

Red Flags to Watch For:

  • Urgency or threats: "Act now or your account will be suspended!" — legitimate services rarely use this language
  • Suspicious sender addresses: Check the actual email address, not just the display name (e.g., "ECU IT" but from randomaddress@gmail.com)
  • Generic greetings: "Dear Student" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name
  • Spelling and grammar errors: Professional organisations proofread their communications
  • Suspicious links: Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking — does it match the claimed sender?
  • Requests for sensitive information: Universities and banks never ask for passwords via email

What to Do If You Receive a Suspicious Message:

  1. Don't click any links or download attachments
  2. Report it to your IT department or email provider
  3. If unsure, contact the supposed sender through official channels
  4. Delete the message
Already clicked a suspicious link? Immediately change your password, enable MFA if not already active, and report the incident to your university IT helpdesk.

Prompt Software Updates

Why it matters: Software updates aren't just about new features — they often contain critical security patches that fix known vulnerabilities. Delaying updates leaves your devices exposed to attacks that exploit these weaknesses.

What to do: Enable automatic updates wherever possible. When you receive an update notification, install it as soon as practical — don't dismiss it repeatedly.

What to Keep Updated:

  • Operating system: Windows, macOS, iOS, Android — these updates are critical and should be installed promptly
  • Web browsers: Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge — browsers are a primary target for attacks
  • Applications: Especially Microsoft Office, Adobe products, and any software that handles sensitive data
  • Antivirus/security software: Only effective if it has the latest threat definitions
  • Mobile apps: Update apps from the official app store, not from random links
  • Router firmware: Often forgotten, but your home router needs updates too

Best Practices:

  • Enable automatic updates on all devices
  • Check for updates at least weekly if automatic updates aren't available
  • Restart your devices regularly to complete pending updates
  • Only download software and updates from official sources
Pro Tip: Schedule a weekly "update check" — maybe Sunday evening before the new week starts. It only takes a few minutes and can prevent major headaches.

Additional Security Tips for Students

Quick Wins for Better Security

Beyond the Core 4, here are additional practices that enhance your digital security:

  • Lock your devices: Always lock your laptop, tablet, or phone when stepping away, even briefly
  • Use a VPN on public Wi-Fi: Coffee shop or airport Wi-Fi? Use a VPN to encrypt your connection
  • Back up your data: Regular backups protect against ransomware and device failure
  • Review app permissions: Does that game really need access to your contacts and location?
  • Be cautious with USB devices: Don't plug in unknown USB drives — they can contain malware
  • Log out of shared computers: Always log out and clear the browser when using library or lab computers
