Trusted Resource Library

Expand your cybersecurity knowledge beyond CyberBridge with these trusted external resources. We've curated links to authoritative organisations that provide reliable, up-to-date information on digital security. Below, you'll also find our comprehensive glossary of essential cybersecurity terms.

External Cybersecurity Organisations

These organisations provide authoritative guidance on cybersecurity best practices. All links open in a new tab.

Australian Cyber Security Centre (ACSC)

Australia's lead agency for national cybersecurity. Provides alerts, advice, and guidance for individuals and organisations.

SANS Institute

World-renowned cybersecurity training and certification organisation. Offers free resources including newsletters and reading room articles.

Have I Been Pwned

Free service to check if your email or password has appeared in known data breaches. Essential tool for personal security monitoring.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework

Comprehensive framework for managing and reducing cybersecurity risk. Industry-standard reference used by organisations worldwide.

StaySafeOnline

Consumer-focused cybersecurity resources from the National Cyber Security Alliance. Great for practical, everyday security tips.

eSafety Commissioner

Australia's independent regulator for online safety. Provides resources for dealing with cyberbullying, image-based abuse, and online harassment.

Cybersecurity Glossary

Understanding cybersecurity terminology is the first step to protecting yourself online. Here's a comprehensive glossary of essential terms every student should know.

Authentication
The process of verifying the identity of a user, device, or system. This typically involves something you know (password), something you have (phone), or something you are (biometrics).
Botnet
A network of compromised computers (called "bots" or "zombies") controlled by an attacker. Botnets are used for distributed denial-of-service attacks, spam distribution, and credential theft.
Brute Force Attack
A method of cracking passwords by systematically trying every possible combination until the correct one is found. Strong passwords and account lockouts help prevent these attacks.
Data Breach
An incident where sensitive, protected, or confidential data is copied, transmitted, viewed, stolen, or used by an unauthorised individual. Breaches can expose personal information, passwords, and financial details.
Encryption
The process of converting information into a code to prevent unauthorised access. Encrypted data can only be read by someone with the correct decryption key. HTTPS uses encryption to secure web traffic.
Firewall
A network security system that monitors and controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules. Acts as a barrier between trusted and untrusted networks.
Keylogger
Malicious software or hardware that records keystrokes on a device. Used to capture passwords, credit card numbers, and other sensitive information as users type.
Malware
Short for "malicious software" — any software intentionally designed to cause damage to a computer, server, client, or network. Includes viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware, and spyware.
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
A security process that requires more than one form of verification to access an account. Typically combines something you know (password) with something you have (phone) or something you are (fingerprint). Also called Two-Factor Authentication (2FA).
Phishing
A type of social engineering attack where attackers impersonate trusted entities to trick victims into revealing sensitive information such as login credentials, credit card numbers, or personal data. Commonly delivered via email.
Ransomware
A type of malware that encrypts a victim's files and demands payment (usually in cryptocurrency) for the decryption key. Can render computers and networks completely unusable.
Social Engineering
Psychological manipulation techniques used to trick people into making security mistakes or giving away sensitive information. Exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities.
Spyware
Software that secretly gathers information about a person or organisation and sends it to another party without consent. Can monitor browsing habits, capture keystrokes, and steal personal data.
Trojan Horse
Malware disguised as legitimate software. Unlike viruses, trojans don't replicate themselves but create a backdoor for attackers to access your system. Often distributed through email attachments or fake downloads.
Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)
See Multi-Factor Authentication. A specific type of MFA that requires exactly two forms of verification—typically a password and a code sent to your phone.
Virtual Private Network (VPN)
A service that creates an encrypted connection between your device and a server, protecting your internet traffic from interception. Essential when using public Wi-Fi networks.
Virus
A type of malware that attaches itself to legitimate programs and spreads when those programs are shared. Unlike worms, viruses require human action (like opening a file) to spread.
Worm
Self-replicating malware that spreads across networks without human intervention. Worms can consume bandwidth, delete files, or install backdoors for future access.
Zero-Day Vulnerability
A software security flaw unknown to the vendor or developer, with no available patch. Called "zero-day" because developers have had zero days to fix the issue before it's exploited.
